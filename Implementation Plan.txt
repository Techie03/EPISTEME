Implementation Plan

Episteme 🔬
The Universal Research Intelligence & Truth Verification Layer

Works on every research paper, across every domain, on every platform — arXiv, PubMed, IEEE, Nature, JSTOR, SSRN, ACM, bioRxiv, Google Scholar, any PDF in your browser.


The Unified Vision
HalluciGuard asked "is this true?"
ArXiv Lens asked "what does this connect to?"
Episteme asks both — simultaneously — for every paper ever written.

Core Modules
🔴 Module 1: Claim Verification Engine
HalluciGuard, but deeper

Extracts every factual claim from the paper using NLP
RAG pipeline cross-checks each claim against 200M+ papers (Semantic Scholar + OpenAlex APIs)
Scores each sentence: Verified / Unverified / Contradicted
Highlights citation misuse — where authors cite a paper that doesn't actually support their claim
Detects statistical red flags: p-hacking, underpowered studies, cherry-picked timeframes, misleading confidence intervals


🟡 Module 2: Research Intelligence Layer
ArXiv Lens, expanded to all domains

Semantic paper map: clusters 50+ related papers visually by concept proximity
Cross-domain connector: finds unexpected links (e.g. this oncology paper connects to a 2019 graph theory paper)
Research gap radar: what questions this paper raises but doesn't answer
Hypothesis engine: RAG + LLM generates 3 novel research directions nobody has formally studied
SOTA tracker: for CS/ML papers, live benchmark leaderboard pulled from Papers With Code


🟢 Module 3: Trust & Integrity Scanner
Brand new — the layer nobody has built

Retraction alert: flags if the paper or any paper it cites has been retracted (via Retraction Watch API)
Reproducibility score: based on code availability, data sharing, methodology detail, pre-registration
Conflict of interest detector: analyzes funding disclosures and author affiliations for bias signals
Peer review transparency: shows review history if available (PLoS, F1000, OpenReview)


🔵 Module 4: Personal Research Memory
Your knowledge, compounded

Every paper you read gets embedded into a local FAISS store
Natural language search: "that immunology paper about gut bacteria I read in March"
Concept knowledge graph built across everything you've ever read
"You might have missed": surfaces a paper from your history that's relevant to what you're reading now
